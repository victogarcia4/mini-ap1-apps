Nervous System Organization
The nervous system is built from neurons and neuroglia, organized into the central nervous system (brain & spinal cord) and the peripheral nervous system (all the nerves that branch out from them).

What the Nervous System Is Made Of

Nervous Tissue Composition

Neurons

Nerve cells that generate and transmit bioelectric signals (impulses) via dendrites and axons.

Neurons receive information through dendrites and send signals away through a single axon. Signals cross gaps called synapses to reach the next cell.
Click to expand

Neuroglia

Accessory ("glue") cells that support, nourish, insulate, and protect neurons — they don't transmit impulses.

Different neuroglial cell types perform different jobs: producing myelin, providing structural support, and defending against pathogens (covered in depth in App 10.4).
Click to expand

Blood Vessels

Supply neural tissue with oxygen and glucose — neurons have very high, constant metabolic demand.

Because neurons can't store much energy, even brief interruptions in blood flow (as in a stroke) can cause rapid, serious damage.
Click to expand

Connective Tissue

Wraps and bundles nerves and nervous system organs, providing structural support and protection.

This is a general category distinct from neuroglia — it's the same family of wrapping/binding tissue found throughout the body (compare to the epimysium/perimysium wrapping muscle in Ch 9).

Two Major Divisions

Central vs. Peripheral Nervous System

Central Nervous System (CNS)

StructuresBrain and spinal cord
LocationEncased in the skull and vertebral column
Key cell typeInterneurons (integration/decision-making)
FunctionInterprets incoming signals and decides on a response

Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)

StructuresCranial nerves (from the brain) and spinal nerves (from the cord)
LocationExtends throughout the rest of the body
Key cell typesSensory (afferent) and motor (efferent) neurons
FunctionCarries signals into and out of the CNS
The CNS is the body's decision-maker; the PNS is its communication network, carrying information to and from the CNS.

Reference — The Human Nervous System

The CNS (brain + spinal cord, shown in the torso/head) is the command center. The PNS (branching nerve network) connects the CNS to every limb and organ, carrying sensory information in and motor commands out.

Reference — A Withdrawal Reflex

Touching a hot surface triggers a sensory receptor, which sends an impulse along a sensory neuron to the spinal cord. An interneuron there integrates the signal and activates a motor neuron, which signals the biceps to contract and pull the hand away.
