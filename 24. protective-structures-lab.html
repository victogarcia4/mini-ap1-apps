Three Lines of Defense
The brain and spinal cord are protected by bone (skull & vertebrae), membranes (the three meninges), and fluid (cerebrospinal fluid) working together as nested shields.

The Cranium (Skull)

8 bones

Eight cranial bones fuse into a rigid case that encloses and protects the brain.

The cranial bones surround the brain within the cranial cavity. They form an immovable, box-like vault — the strongest bony protection in the body — shielding the ~100 billion neurons of the brain from impact.

The Vertebral Column

26 bones

Twenty-six vertebrae stack into a flexible bony tube that encases the spinal cord.

Each vertebra has a central opening (the vertebral foramen); stacked together these form the vertebral canal that houses the spinal cord. Unlike the fused skull, the column stays flexible so the trunk can bend while still shielding the cord.

Reference — Cranial Meninges in Cross-Section

From outside in: skull bone → dura mater → arachnoid mater → subarachnoid space (filled with CSF, crossed by arachnoid trabeculae) → pia mater clinging to the folded cortex. The CSF-filled subarachnoid space is the brain's built-in shock absorber.

Protecting the Spinal Cord

A Vertebra in Cross-Section — Click a Structure

Vertebral Foramen
The central opening of each vertebra. Stacked, these openings form the vertebral canal that houses and protects the spinal cord.

Reference — Spinal Cord Within a Vertebra

The spinal cord sits inside the vertebral foramen, wrapped by the same three meninges as the brain. Note the key spinal difference: the dura is not fused to bone here, leaving an epidural space (fat & loose connective tissue) between dura and vertebra.
