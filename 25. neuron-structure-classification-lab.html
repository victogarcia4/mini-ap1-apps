Neuron Guide

The neuron is the structural and functional unit of the nervous system. Explore its parts, how it is insulated, and the two ways neurons are classified.

The Neuron
Every neuron has three basic parts: a cell body (soma), one or more dendrites that receive signals, and a single axon that carries the impulse away.

Cell Body (Soma / Perikaryon)
The central portion of the neuron. It holds the nucleus, a prominent nucleolus, and Nissl bodies, and contains all the usual organelles except centrioles.

Reference — Structure of a Multipolar Neuron

Signal direction runs one way: dendrites → cell body → axon hillock → down the axon → axon terminals (synaptic knobs). The gaps in the myelin are the nodes of Ranvier.

Insulating the Axon

CNS Myelination

Myelin-forming cellOligodendrocytes
One cell wrapsSeveral axons at once
Neurilemma?No (no continuous outer sheath)
LocationBrain and spinal cord

PNS Myelination

Myelin-forming cellSchwann cells
One cell wrapsOne segment of one axon
Neurilemma?Yes (Schwann cytoplasm + nuclei)
LocationPeripheral nerves
Large axons are wrapped in a myelin sheath (a lipoprotein insulation); small axons are unmyelinated. In the PNS, all axons — myelinated or not — are still associated with Schwann cells.

Myelin Sheath

A fatty (lipoprotein) insulating layer wrapped in many layers around large axons.

Myelin speeds up impulse conduction and insulates the axon. In the PNS it is made by Schwann cells; in the CNS by oligodendrocytes.

Neurilemma

The outer layer of Schwann cell cytoplasm and nuclei surrounding the myelin sheath (PNS).

Also called the neurolemmal sheath. Because it survives injury, the neurilemma helps guide regeneration of damaged PNS axons — something CNS axons (lacking it) generally can't do.

Nodes of Ranvier

The gaps in the myelin sheath between adjacent Schwann cells.

At these bare spots the axon membrane is exposed. Impulses jump from node to node, which is why myelinated fibers conduct far faster (covered in App 10.5).
Click to expand

Schwann Cells

The neuroglial cells that produce myelin in the peripheral nervous system.

Each Schwann cell wraps a single segment of one axon in many layers. Between neighboring Schwann cells lie the nodes of Ranvier.

Two Kinds of Nervous Tissue

White Matter vs. Gray Matter

White Matter

Made ofBundles of myelinated nerve fibers (axons)
Why "white"?The lipid (fatty) myelin looks white
Main jobCarries signals between regions — the "cables"

Gray Matter

Made ofBundles of cell bodies (or unmyelinated fibers)
Why "gray"?No myelin, so no white coloring
Main jobIntegration & processing — the "computers"
Simple rule: white matter = myelinated axons (the wiring), while gray matter = neuron cell bodies (where processing happens). Myelin is what makes the difference in color.

Classifying Neurons by Shape

Structural Classes — Click a Type

Bipolar Neuron
Two extensions: one fused dendrite leading toward the cell body and one axon leading away. Found in special sense organs — the eyes, nose, and ears.

Reference — Comparison of Neuron Structural Types

Neurons are grouped by how many processes leave the cell body: bipolar (two), unipolar/pseudounipolar (one that splits into peripheral and central branches), and multipolar (many dendrites + one axon).
