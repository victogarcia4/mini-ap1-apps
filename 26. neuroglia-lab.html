Neuroglia Guide

Neuroglia ("nerve glue") are the accessory cells that support, feed, insulate, and defend neurons. There are four types in the CNS and two in the PNS.

Neuroglia = "Nerve Glue"
Glia don't transmit impulses. Instead they form a supporting network around neurons — providing structure, nourishment, myelin, immune defense, and a stable environment.

Four Glia of the Brain & Spinal Cord

CNS Neuroglia — Click a Cell

Astrocyte
Star-shaped and the most abundant glial cell. It nourishes neurons and provides structural support, physically linking neurons to their blood supply.

Reference — The Four CNS Neuroglia

The four CNS glia: astrocyte (support & nourishment), oligodendrocyte (myelin), microglia (phagocytosis/immune defense), and ependymal cells (line CSF cavities; cilia help circulate CSF).

Two Glia of the Peripheral Nerves

PNS Neuroglia — Click a Cell

Schwann Cell
Produces the myelin sheath around peripheral axons (and also envelops unmyelinated axons). It forms the neurilemma, which enables PNS axons to regenerate after injury.

Reference — The Two PNS Neuroglia

The two PNS glia: Schwann cells (myelinate or envelop peripheral axons; form the neurilemma) and satellite cells (surround and support neuron cell bodies within ganglia, regulating their environment).

All Six Neuroglia Compared

Six Glia, Six Jobs

Astrocyte

CNS

Shape hook: star-shaped ⭐

Nourishes neurons & provides structural support; links neurons to blood vessels.

Oligodendrocyte

CNS

Shape hook: eyeball-like 👁

Produces myelin in the CNS — one cell wraps several axons at once.

Microglia

CNS

Shape hook: spider-like 🕷

Phagocytosis — the CNS immune cell; engulfs pathogens and cellular debris.

Ependyma

CNS

Shape hook: ciliated sheet 〰

Lines the cavities of the CNS (ventricles & central canal); cilia help circulate CSF.

Schwann Cell

PNS

Shape hook: wraps one axon segment 🌀

Produces myelin in the PNS; forms the neurilemma that guides axon regeneration.

Satellite Cell

PNS

Shape hook: shell around a cell body 🔵

Surrounds & supports neuron cell bodies inside ganglia; regulates their environment.

Quick recall: The two myelin-makers are the Oligodendrocyte (CNS) and the Schwann cell (PNS) — remember "Oligo = Our brain's insulator." Microglia = Mini immune cells. Ependyma = lines the CSF-filled spaces. Astrocyte = the Abundant All-rounder that feeds and supports.

Can a Damaged Neuron Recover?

Cell Body Injury vs. Axon Injury

Cell Body Injury

✗ Fatal — the neuron dies

The cell body (soma) holds the nucleus and life-support machinery. If it is destroyed, the entire neuron dies — and because mature neurons generally do not divide, it usually cannot be replaced.

Axon Injury

✓ May regenerate

If only the axon is damaged but the cell body survives, the axon may regenerate. In the PNS, the surviving neurilemma (Schwann-cell sheath) forms a tube that guides the regrowing axon back to its target.

Key link to App 10.3: PNS axons regenerate largely because Schwann cells provide the neurilemma. CNS axons (myelinated by oligodendrocytes, which form no neurilemma) generally do not regenerate well.
