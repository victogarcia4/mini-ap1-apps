Nerve Impulse Guide

A neuron sends messages as brief electrical signals. Explore how it stays charged at rest, fires an action potential, and speeds that impulse down the axon.

The nerve impulse is an action potential — a wave of voltage change that sweeps along the axon. It begins from a polarized resting state, spikes when a stimulus reaches threshold, then resets.

Inside is negative relative to the outside — the membrane is "polarized."
Sodium–Potassium Pump (Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase)
An active transporter that pumps 3 Na⁺ out for every 2 K⁺ it brings in, using ATP. This unequal exchange helps build and maintain the charge difference across the membrane.

The Action Potential — An All-or-None Spike

Animation — One Action Potential

A stimulus drives the membrane to threshold (−55 mV); Na⁺ floods in (depolarization, upward spike), then K⁺ flows out (repolarization) and the membrane returns toward rest.

1 · Resting & Threshold
The membrane sits at −70 mV until a stimulus depolarizes it to about −55 mV — the threshold. Reach threshold and an action potential fires completely; fail to reach it and nothing happens (all-or-none).

Reference — Voltage-Gated Na⁺ & K⁺ Channels

Voltage-gated Na⁺ channels open first (depolarization), then inactivate as voltage-gated K⁺ channels open (repolarization). Their timing produces the spike's shape.

Saltatory conduction (myelinated): the impulse leaps from one node of Ranvier to the next, skipping the insulated internodes. This is fast and energy-efficient.

Reference — Saltatory vs. Continuous Conduction

Two factors speed conduction: myelination (impulse jumps node to node) and larger axon diameter. Bare, thin axons conduct slowest.

Refractory Periods & The Big Picture

When Can a Neuron Fire Again?

Absolute Refractory Period

✗ Cannot fire again — at all

While the voltage-gated Na⁺ channels are open or inactivated, no stimulus — however strong — can trigger another action potential. This guarantees each impulse is a separate, one-way event and sets the maximum firing rate.

Relative Refractory Period

◐ Can fire — but only with a strong stimulus

As the membrane repolarizes and hyperpolarizes, the neuron can fire again, but only if an unusually strong stimulus pushes it back to threshold. Excitability gradually returns to normal.

Because the region behind the impulse is refractory, the action potential can only move forward — away from the cell body, toward the axon terminals.

Quick recall: A neuron rests polarized at −70 mV. A stimulus to −55 mV (threshold) fires an all-or-none spike: Na⁺ in (depolarize) → K⁺ out (repolarize) → brief undershoot → reset. The refractory period keeps it one-way, and myelin + a fat axon make it fast.
