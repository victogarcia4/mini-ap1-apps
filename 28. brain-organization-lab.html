Brain Guide

The brain is the largest, most complex part of the nervous system — about 100 billion neurons overseeing the whole body. Explore its four major parts and what each one does.

Four major parts: the cerebrum (thinking, sensation, voluntary movement), the diencephalon (thalamus + hypothalamus at the core), the brain stem (automatic survival functions), and the cerebellum (coordination & balance).

The Cerebrum & Its Four Lobes

Click a Lobe of the Cerebrum

Frontal Lobe
The most anterior lobe. Home to the primary motor cortex (voluntary movement), Broca's area (motor speech), and centers for reasoning, judgment, and personality.

Corpus Callosum

A deep bridge of white matter connecting the left & right hemispheres so they can communicate.

Gyri & Sulci

Gyri are the raised ridges (convolutions); sulci are the shallow grooves. The central sulcus divides frontal & parietal.

Fissures

Deep grooves: the longitudinal fissure separates the hemispheres; the transverse fissure separates cerebrum from cerebellum.

Gray vs White

The outer cortex is gray matter (neuron cell bodies); the inner bulk is white matter (myelinated axons).

Functional Areas of the Cerebral Cortex

Sensory, Motor & Association Areas — Click to Explore

Primary Motor Cortex
Located in the precentral gyrus of the frontal lobe (just anterior to the central sulcus). Initiates all voluntary skeletal muscle movements.

Reference — Functional Areas of the Cortex

Sensory areas (somatosensory, visual, auditory, gustatory) receive input; motor areas (primary motor, Broca's) send commands; association areas interpret and connect everything.

Hemisphere dominance: both hemispheres share basic sensory & motor control through the corpus callosum, but one — usually the left — dominates language, math, and logic; the other handles spatial orientation, art, and music. Memory: learning is acquiring knowledge; memory is keeping it. Short- and long-term memory involve the hippocampus and amygdala.

Deep Brain — Diencephalon, Limbic System & Basal Nuclei

The Core of the Brain — Click a Structure

Thalamus
A gray-matter mass in the diencephalon. The central relay station for incoming sensory impulses (except smell) — it directs each signal to the correct area of the cerebral cortex.

Reference — Midsagittal View of the Brain

A midsagittal cut reveals the deep structures: the thalamus and hypothalamus sit at the core (diencephalon), just below the corpus callosum.

Brain Stem & Cerebellum

Automatic Control & Coordination — Click a Structure

Midbrain
The superior part of the brain stem, between the diencephalon and pons. Its corpora quadrigemina mediate visual and auditory reflexes; it also contains reticular formation.

Reference — Brain Stem & Cerebellum (Midsagittal)

The brain stem (midbrain → pons → medulla) blends into the spinal cord; the cerebellum sits behind it, below the occipital lobe (separated by the transverse fissure).

Big picture: the cerebrum thinks, senses, and moves you; the diencephalon (thalamus relays, hypothalamus balances) sits at the core; the brain stem (midbrain · pons · medulla) runs automatic survival jobs; and the cerebellum coordinates movement and keeps you balanced.
